Answers to frequently asked questions

Broadly speaking, a psychologist is a professional who helps people cope with their thoughts, feelings and behavior. In psychotherapy, you can improve the quality of your life: improve relationships with people, find the source of problems and anxiety, and study yourself.

Psychotherapists work on Yasno, but for convenience we use the words “psychologist” and “psychotherapist” as synonyms.

A psychologist listens to your request or helps you formulate it - and then carefully guides you, helping you understand yourself or the situation.

Many specialists clearly work with the fine-tuning of the human psyche: defenses, the unconscious, attitudes, internal conflicts - and how all this affects everyday life.

You need a psychologist if you want to put your thoughts and feelings in order.
For example, your relationship with your partner is not working out. Or you just can’t “find yourself” and work gets annoying. Or it seems that only bad things await in the future - and this makes you anxious. In sessions with a therapist, you can talk about this, explore past experiences, discover negative thinking patterns and learn to respond to situations differently.

There are many directions in therapy, and it is easy to get lost in them. Different approaches pay more attention to different aspects: the study of childhood, experiences in the moment “here and now,” bodily reactions, and so on.

But in fact, it’s not the method that works—it’s the contact with the therapist. During the process, you should feel that you are being listened to carefully, not judged or judged, and that you are interested.

You can work independently with a psychologist from the age of 16. If you are younger, you can call the helpline or write to the psychological support chat - we have collected contacts here. It's anonymous and free.

There are cases when the issue is an 'urgent' situation: 'I want to break up with my partner — but I’m scared.' You can tell if the psychologist helped by whether the specific problem was resolved.

But sometimes the issue we bring to the psychologist is just the tip of the iceberg. In that case, therapy becomes a long process, with room for both progress and resistance. Over time, you'll notice changes: for example, you’ll learn to say 'no,' stop idealizing those around you, and allow yourself to make mistakes sometimes.
